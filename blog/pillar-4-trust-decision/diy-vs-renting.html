DIY vs. Renting: Why Renting Event Equipment Saves You Money

When you are planning an event, the temptation to buy your own tables, chairs, and tent can seem like a smart move, especially if you host a few events a year. But once you factor in the real costs of purchasing, transporting, storing, and maintaining event equipment, renting almost always wins. Let us break down the actual numbers side by side.

The True Cost of Buying Event Equipment

When people think about buying party supplies, they usually only consider the sticker price. But the real cost goes far beyond what you pay at the store. Here is what buying actually looks like for a 50-guest event:

Buying Chairs: $1,000 - $2,000+

  • 50 basic folding chairs: $20 to $40 each = $1,000 to $2,000
  • Transport: You will need a truck or large SUV for 50 chairs. Truck rental runs $40 to $100 per trip.
  • Storage: 50 folding chairs take up roughly 25 square feet of garage or storage space. A storage unit in San Diego runs $75 to $150 per month.
  • Wear and tear: Cheap folding chairs break, get dirty, and look worn after 2-3 uses. Quality event chairs cost more upfront.

Buying Tables: $560 - $1,500+

  • 7 folding tables (6ft): $80 to $150 each = $560 to $1,050
  • Transport: Tables do not fit in most cars. You need a truck or trailer, and loading/unloading 7 tables is a two-person job.
  • Storage: Seven 6-foot tables stacked need significant wall space in a garage.
  • Quality gap: Retail folding tables are lighter-gauge than commercial rental tables. They wobble, sag, and do not look as polished.

Buying a Tent: $500 - $2,000+

  • A 10x20 pop-up canopy (retail): $150 to $400
  • A 20x20 event tent (frame or pole): $500 to $2,000
  • Setup: Larger tents require 3-4 people and 1-2 hours to assemble safely. You need anchors, stakes, or weights.
  • Storage: A 20x20 tent in its bag takes up an entire closet or significant garage space.
  • Lifespan: UV exposure, wind, and moisture degrade tent fabric. Most consumer-grade tents last 3-5 events before looking faded or developing tears.

The Hidden Costs Most People Forget

The sticker price is just the beginning. Here are the costs that catch DIY buyers off guard:

1. Storage Space

In San Diego, the average self-storage unit costs $130 per month for a 5x10 space. If you are storing 50 chairs, 7 tables, and a tent, you need at least that size. Over 12 months, that is $1,560 per year just to store items you might use once or twice.

2. Transport Vehicle

Unless you own a truck, you will need to rent one every time you move this equipment. Home Depot and U-Haul charge $20 to $40 for a cargo van, plus mileage. And you will need at least two trips: one to deliver, one to pick up.

3. Setup Time and Labor

Setting up a tent, arranging 50 chairs, and positioning 7 tables takes 3 to 4 hours with a helper. On your event day, that is time you should be spending on food prep, decorations, or simply relaxing before guests arrive. With our white-glove delivery and setup, our team handles everything while you focus on what matters.

4. Quality and Appearance

Retail folding chairs and tables look fine in a store. After two events outdoors in San Diego sun, they start showing wear: faded colors, wobbly legs, scratched surfaces. Rental-grade equipment is commercial quality, built to look pristine event after event. Our chair rentals and table rentals arrive in like-new condition every time.

5. Cleanup and Maintenance

After an event, every chair needs to be wiped down, every table cleaned, and the tent dried before storage (to prevent mold). That is another 2 to 3 hours of work. When you rent, we pick everything up and handle all the cleaning.

When Does Buying Make Sense?

We want to be honest: there are a few situations where buying might make sense.

  • You host 15+ events per year with the same setup. At that frequency, the per-event cost of ownership starts to compete with rental costs.
  • You have free storage space (a large garage, barn, or warehouse) and a truck for transport.
  • You only need a few items. Buying 4 folding chairs for your patio is obviously cheaper than renting. We are talking about event-scale quantities here.

For most people hosting 1 to 4 events per year, renting is the clear winner on cost, convenience, and quality.
